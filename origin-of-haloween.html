The Celtic Roots of Samhain

An Ancient Harvest

Halloween's origins date back to the ancient Celtic festival of **Samhain** (pronounced *sow-in*). The Celts, who lived 2,000 years ago, mostly in the area that is now Ireland, the United Kingdom, and northern France, celebrated their new year on November 1. This day marked the end of summer and the harvest and the beginning of the dark, cold winter, a time of year that was often associated with death.

Bridging Worlds

The Celts believed that on the night before the new year, the boundary between the worlds of the living and the dead became blurred. They celebrated Samhain on the evening of October 31. They thought that the ghosts of the dead returned to earth. This supernatural event inspired them to light **bonfires**, wear costumes, and tell fortunes to ward off evil spirits.

Modern Celebrations

Trick-or-Treating

This tradition can be traced to medieval practices where the poor would beg for food, and in return, they would pray for the dead relatives of the household. It evolved into children going door-to-door asking for sweets.

Costumes & Masks

Originally, people wore costumes to confuse or mimic the roaming spirits of Samhain. Today, costumes are largely secular and include everything from pop culture icons to classic monsters.

Jack-O'-Lanterns

The carving of pumpkins originated from an Irish myth about a man named Stingy Jack. He tricked the Devil and was cursed to wander the Earth with only a carved turnip and a lump of coal for light.
